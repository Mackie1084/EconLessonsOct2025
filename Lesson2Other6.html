🤔 What is Price Elasticity of Demand?

Price elasticity of demand measures how responsive consumers are to price changes.

📐 Formula:

PED = % Change in Quantity Demanded / % Change in Price

🏃‍♂️

Elastic (PED > 1)

Consumers are very responsive to price changes

Example: Luxury cars, dining out

🐌

Inelastic (PED < 1)

Consumers are not very responsive to price changes

Example: Rice, electricity, gasoline

⚖️

Unitary (PED = 1)

Price and quantity changes are proportional

Example: Some consumer goods

🌏 Singapore vs India: Market Context

Income levels and market conditions create different elasticity patterns:

💰 High Income Economy

• GDP per capita: ~$70,000 USD

• More elastic demand for luxury goods

• Less sensitive to basic necessities pricing

• Strong substitution effects

🏙️ Market Characteristics

• Small island nation with limited space

• High dependence on imports

• Government controls (COE, HDB)

• Sophisticated consumer base

🏭 Emerging Economy

• GDP per capita: ~$2,400 USD

• More inelastic demand for basic goods

• Price-sensitive consumer behavior

• Large informal economy

🌾 Market Characteristics

• Large agricultural population

• Income inequality variations

• Regional price differences

• Government subsidies on essentials

🎯 Factors Affecting Price Elasticity

Several factors determine how elastic demand will be:

🔄

Availability of Substitutes

More substitutes = More elastic

Singapore: Many food options in hawker centers

India: Limited substitutes for staples like rice

💰

Income Level

Higher income = More elastic for luxuries

Singapore: Less concerned about basic food prices

India: Very sensitive to essential goods pricing

⏱️

Time Period

Long-run = More elastic than short-run

Both countries: Consumers adjust habits over time

❤️

Necessity vs Luxury

Necessities = Inelastic, Luxuries = Elastic

Context varies: Cars are luxury in India, necessity in Singapore

📊

Proportion of Income

Higher % of income = More elastic

India: Food takes larger income share

Singapore: Housing takes largest share

🏠

Brand Loyalty

Strong loyalty = Less elastic

Both countries: Premium brands command loyalty

🏛️ Government Policy Applications

How governments use elasticity concepts in policy making:

Singapore Policies

🚗 COE System: Elastic demand allows effective quantity control

🚭 Tobacco Tax: High elasticity reduces consumption effectively

🏠 HDB Pricing: Inelastic housing demand allows controlled increases

⛽ Petrol Tax: Moderate elasticity balances revenue and consumption

India Policies

🌾 Food Subsidies: Inelastic demand justifies price support

⛽ Fuel Subsidies: Inelastic demand creates fiscal burden

📱 GST on Phones: Elastic demand led to rate reductions

🚗 Auto Policy: High elasticity influenced import duty structure

📊 Policy Impact Analysis

Understanding elasticity helps predict policy outcomes:

✅ Effective Tax Policies

• Elastic goods: Taxes reduce consumption (sin taxes)

• Inelastic goods: Taxes generate revenue (fuel, utilities)

❌ Policy Challenges

• Elastic goods: Subsidies create fiscal burden

• Inelastic goods: Price controls create shortages
