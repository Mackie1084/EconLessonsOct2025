1. Consumer Choice

Consumer choice theory explains how individuals allocate their limited resources among various goods and services to maximize satisfaction (utility).

2. Consumers in Markets

This topic examines how consumers interact with markets and how their behavior aggregates to form market demand.

Individual vs. Market Demand

Individual Demand

Shows the quantity a single consumer will purchase at various prices, holding other factors constant.

Factors affecting individual demand: Income, preferences, prices of related goods, expectations

Market Demand

The horizontal sum of all individual demand curves in the market.

Qmarket = Q‚ÇÅ + Q‚ÇÇ + Q‚ÇÉ + ... + Q‚Çô

Consumer Surplus

The difference between what consumers are willing to pay and what they actually pay represents consumer surplus‚Äîa measure of consumer welfare.

Consumer Surplus = Willingness to Pay - Actual Price

üìå Market Example

In a market with 1,000 consumers, if each demands 2 units at $5, the market demand at $5 is 2,000 units. As price falls to $3, if each consumer now demands 4 units, market demand rises to 4,000 units.

Normal Goods

Demand increases as income rises (e.g., organic food, new cars)

Inferior Goods

Demand decreases as income rises (e.g., instant noodles, used goods)

4. Production and Cost

Production theory analyzes how firms transform inputs (factors of production) into outputs, and how costs relate to production levels.

The Production Function

Q = f(L, K, M, ...)

Output = function of (Labor, Capital, Materials, ...)

Types of Costs

Fixed Costs (FC)

Costs that don't vary with output level

Examples: Rent, insurance, salaries of permanent staff, equipment depreciation

Variable Costs (VC)

Costs that change with output level

Examples: Raw materials, hourly wages, utilities, shipping costs

üìä Total Cost Components

TC = FC + VC
ATC = TC/Q = AFC + AVC
MC = ŒîTC/ŒîQ

ATC = Average Total Cost | AFC = Average Fixed Cost | AVC = Average Variable Cost | MC = Marginal Cost

Economic vs. Accounting Costs

Accounting Costs: Explicit, out-of-pocket expenses

Economic Costs: Accounting costs + opportunity costs (implicit costs)

üìå Example

A bakery owner uses her own building. The accounting cost includes ingredients, wages, and utilities. The economic cost also includes the rental income she could have earned by leasing the building‚Äîan opportunity cost.

5. Production and Cost in Short-Run

In the short-run, at least one input (typically capital) is fixed, while others (typically labor) are variable.

Key Production Concepts

Total, Average, and Marginal Product

TP = Total Product (total output)
AP = TP/L (Average Product of Labor)
MP = ŒîTP/ŒîL (Marginal Product of Labor)

Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns

As more units of a variable input are added to fixed inputs, the marginal product eventually declines.

üìå Classic Example: Restaurant Kitchen

1st worker: MP = 20 meals/hour

2nd worker: MP = 25 meals/hour (specialization benefits)

3rd worker: MP = 18 meals/hour (starting to crowd)

4th worker: MP = 12 meals/hour (too crowded, inefficient)

The kitchen space (fixed input) limits how productive additional workers can be.

Short-Run Cost Curves

Key Relationships

‚úì AFC continuously declines

‚úì AVC is U-shaped

‚úì ATC is U-shaped

‚úì MC intersects AVC and ATC at their minimums

Why U-Shaped?

Initial decline: Fixed costs spread over more units

Eventually rise: Diminishing returns increase variable costs faster

üîë Critical Insight

When MP is rising, MC is falling. When MP is falling (diminishing returns), MC is rising. There's an inverse relationship between marginal product and marginal cost.

6. Production and Cost in Long-Run

In the long-run, all inputs are variable. Firms can adjust plant size, technology, and all factors of production.

Long-Run Average Cost (LRAC)

The LRAC curve is the envelope of all short-run average cost curves, showing the lowest cost of producing each output level when all inputs can be adjusted.

üìà Economies and Diseconomies of Scale

Economies of Scale (LRAC declining):

‚Ä¢ Specialization and division of labor

‚Ä¢ Bulk purchasing discounts

‚Ä¢ Spreading fixed costs (R&D, advertising)

‚Ä¢ Technological efficiencies

Constant Returns to Scale (LRAC flat):

‚Ä¢ Output increases proportionally with inputs

Diseconomies of Scale (LRAC rising):

‚Ä¢ Management complexity and coordination costs

‚Ä¢ Communication difficulties in large organizations

‚Ä¢ Bureaucratic inefficiencies

üìå Real-World Example: Automobile Manufacturing

Small scale (100 cars/year): High cost per unit - $50,000/car. Limited automation, hand-assembly.

Medium scale (10,000 cars/year): Lower cost - $25,000/car. Assembly line, specialized workers, bulk parts purchasing.

Large scale (500,000 cars/year): Lowest cost - $20,000/car. Full automation, maximum efficiency.

Very large scale (5 million cars/year): Rising cost - $22,000/car. Management becomes unwieldy, coordination issues.

Minimum Efficient Scale (MES)

The lowest output level at which LRAC reaches its minimum. This determines optimal firm size in an industry.

7. Understanding Profits

Profit is the ultimate goal for firms, but economists distinguish between different types of profit.

Types of Profit

Accounting Profit

œÄ = TR - Explicit Costs

Total Revenue minus explicit (out-of-pocket) costs

What appears on financial statements

Economic Profit

œÄ = TR - (Explicit + Implicit Costs)

Total Revenue minus all costs including opportunity costs

True measure of profitability

üéØ Three Profit Scenarios

Economic Profit > 0: Firm earns above-normal returns, attracting new entrants

Economic Profit = 0 (Normal Profit): Firm covers all costs including opportunity costs. No incentive to enter or exit. Long-run equilibrium in perfect competition.

Economic Profit < 0 (Economic Loss): Firm isn't covering opportunity costs. Should consider exiting in long-run.

üìå Detailed Example: Coffee Shop

Revenue: $500,000/year

Explicit costs: $350,000 (rent, wages, supplies, etc.)

Accounting Profit: $150,000

Implicit costs: $120,000 (owner's foregone salary) + $30,000 (return on capital invested)

Economic Profit: $500,000 - $350,000 - $150,000 = $0

Interpretation: The business is making normal profit‚Äîcovering all costs including what the owner could earn elsewhere. No incentive to close, but also not extraordinarily profitable.

8. The Demand Constraints

Firms face demand constraints that limit their pricing and output decisions. Understanding demand is crucial for profit maximization.

Market Structures and Demand

Perfect Competition

Firm's demand: Perfectly elastic (horizontal)

Price taker: P = MR = AR

Can sell any quantity at market price

Imperfect Competition

Firm's demand: Downward sloping

Price maker: Must lower price to sell more

MR < P for all units after the first

Revenue Concepts

üìä Revenue Relationships

Total Revenue (TR) = P √ó Q
Average Revenue (AR) = TR/Q = P
Marginal Revenue (MR) = ŒîTR/ŒîQ

Elasticity and Revenue

Elastic Demand (|E| > 1): Price decrease increases total revenue

Unit Elastic (|E| = 1): Price changes don't affect total revenue

Inelastic Demand (|E| < 1): Price increase increases total revenue

üìå Example: Monopolist's Dilemma

A pharmaceutical company has a patent on a drug. At $100/unit, it sells 1,000 units (TR = $100,000). To sell 1,100 units, it must lower price to $95 for all units (TR = $104,500).

MR = ($104,500 - $100,000) / 100 = $45

Notice MR ($45) is less than price ($95) because the firm must lower price on all units, not just the marginal unit.

üîë Critical Insight

The demand curve facing a firm determines its revenue possibilities and thus constrains profit-maximizing behavior. Firms cannot choose price and quantity independently‚Äîthey're linked by the demand curve.

9. The Profit-Maximizing Output Level

Firms maximize profit where the additional revenue from producing one more unit equals the additional cost.

The Golden Rule of Profit Maximization

‚≠ê MC = MR

Produce where Marginal Cost equals Marginal Revenue

Logic:

‚Ä¢ If MR > MC: Producing another unit adds more to revenue than to cost ‚Üí Increase output

‚Ä¢ If MR < MC: The last unit costs more than it brings in ‚Üí Decrease output

‚Ä¢ If MR = MC: Perfect balance ‚Üí Profit maximized

Application by Market Structure

Perfect Competition

Since P = MR:

P = MC

Produce where price equals marginal cost

Monopoly/Imperfect Competition

Since P > MR:

MR = MC, then find P from demand

Set quantity where MR = MC, then charge maximum price consumers will pay

üìå Numerical Example: Perfectly Competitive Firm

Market price: $50 per unit

Cost structure:

At Q = 100: MC = $40, Profit increases by producing more

At Q = 150: MC = $50, Profit maximized (P = MC)

At Q = 180: MC = $55, Profit decreases by producing more

Optimal output: 150 units

üéØ Additional Conditions

Second-order condition: MC must be rising at the profit-maximizing point

Shutdown condition (short-run): Only produce if P ‚â• AVC

Exit condition (long-run): Only produce if P ‚â• ATC

10. Dealing with Losses

Not all firms make profits. Understanding when to continue operating at a loss versus shutting down is crucial for firm survival.

Short-Run vs. Long-Run Decisions

üè≠ Short-Run Decision: Shutdown Rule

Even with losses, continue operating if:

P ‚â• AVC

or equivalently

TR ‚â• TVC

Logic: If revenue covers variable costs, continuing to operate minimizes losses because it contributes something toward fixed costs. Fixed costs must be paid regardless, so they're irrelevant to the shutdown decision.

Continue Operating

If P ‚â• AVC:

Loss = FC - (TR - TVC)

Smaller loss than shutting down

Example: TR = $8,000, TVC = $6,000, FC = $3,000

Loss = -$1,000 (better than -$3,000)

Shutdown

If P < AVC:

Loss = FC (total fixed costs)

Minimize loss by stopping production

Example: TR = $5,000, TVC = $6,000, FC = $3,000

Loss = -$4,000 operating vs -$3,000 shut down

üè¢ Long-Run Decision: Exit Rule

In the long-run, all costs are variable. Exit the industry if:

P < ATC

or equivalently

TR < TC

Logic: If the firm cannot cover all costs (including opportunity costs) in the long-run, resources should be reallocated to more profitable uses.

üìå Real-World Example: Restaurant During Pandemic

Monthly costs: Rent & equipment = $10,000 (FC), Food & labor = $15,000 (VC)

Monthly revenue: $18,000

Analysis:

‚Ä¢ TR ($18,000) > TVC ($15,000) ‚úì Continue operating short-run

‚Ä¢ TR ($18,000) < TC ($25,000) ‚úó Exit in long-run if conditions don't improve

‚Ä¢ Short-run loss: -$7,000 (vs -$10,000 if shutdown)

Decision: Stay open temporarily, hoping for recovery. If situation persists beyond lease renewal, exit the market.

Break-Even Analysis

Break-even point: Where TR = TC (zero economic profit)

Shutdown point: Where P = minimum AVC

Exit point: Where P = minimum ATC

üí° Strategic Considerations

‚Ä¢ Sunk costs: Past costs that cannot be recovered should be ignored in decisions

‚Ä¢ Fixed costs: Relevant for long-run exit decisions, irrelevant for short-run shutdown

‚Ä¢ Variable costs: Relevant for all decisions‚Äîthe key to short-run shutdown rule

‚Ä¢ Opportunity costs: Always relevant‚Äîwhat else could resources earn?
