Definition of Supply

Supply refers to the quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to offer for sale at various prices during a specific period of time.

‚öñÔ∏è The Law of Supply

The Fundamental Principle

"All else being equal, as the price of a good increases, the quantity supplied increases, and vice versa."

This creates a direct (positive) relationship between price and quantity supplied - they move in the same direction!

üîç Why Do Producers Supply More at Higher Prices?

‚úÖ Profit Incentive

Higher prices mean higher potential profits, motivating producers to increase production and supply more goods to the market.

‚úÖ Cover Higher Costs

As production increases, costs per unit often rise. Higher prices help cover these increasing marginal costs of production.

‚úÖ New Market Entrants

High prices attract new producers to enter the market, increasing the overall market supply.

‚úÖ Resource Allocation

Producers shift resources from less profitable goods to more profitable ones when prices rise.

üìã Supply Schedule

A supply schedule is a table that shows the relationship between the price of a good and the quantity that producers are willing to supply. It lists specific price-quantity combinations.

üìä Reading a Supply Schedule:

  • Each row shows a different price point
  • The quantity column shows how much producers will supply at that price
  • As you read down (prices increase), quantities supplied also increase
  • This demonstrates the Law of Supply in numerical form

üìà Supply Curve

A supply curve is a graphical representation of the supply schedule. It plots price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal axis, showing the direct relationship between them.

üîç Key Characteristics of the Supply Curve:

  • Upward Sloping: The curve rises from left to right, showing that higher prices lead to higher quantities supplied
  • Direct Relationship: Price and quantity move in the same direction
  • Each Point: Represents a specific price-quantity combination from the supply schedule
  • Smooth Curve: In reality, producers can supply at any price point, not just those in our table

üîÑ What Can Shift the Supply Curve?

While price causes movement along the supply curve, other factors cause the entire curve to shift. These are called the determinants of supply.

üè≠ Production Costs

Impact: When costs of labor, raw materials, or energy change

‚Ä¢ Lower costs: Supply increases (curve shifts right)

‚Ä¢ Higher costs: Supply decreases (curve shifts left)

üíª Technology

Impact: Improvements in production methods and technology

‚Ä¢ Better technology: Supply increases (more efficient production)

‚Ä¢ Outdated methods: Supply decreases

Example: Automated assembly lines allow factories to produce more goods at lower costs.

üë• Number of Sellers

‚Ä¢ More sellers: Total supply increases

‚Ä¢ Fewer sellers: Total supply decreases

‚Ä¢ Favorable conditions: Supply increases

üí∞ Taxes and Subsidies

‚Ä¢ Subsidies: Supply increases (government support)
