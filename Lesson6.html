üè≠ Physical Capital & Firm's Investment Decision

What is Physical Capital?

Physical capital refers to tangible assets that firms use in production: machinery, buildings, equipment, technology, and infrastructure.

The Investment Decision Framework

Key Question: Should the firm invest in new capital?

Firms compare:

üíµ Marginal Cost

The cost of acquiring and financing the capital

üí∞ Marginal Benefit

The expected revenue from using the capital

Example: A bakery considering a $50,000 oven must evaluate if the additional bread production will generate enough revenue to cover the cost plus interest payments.
Invest if: Marginal Revenue Product > Cost of Capital

üìà The Firm's Demand for Capital

Determinants of Capital Demand

üìâ Downward Sloping Demand Curve
As the interest rate (cost of capital) decreases, firms demand more capital.

Key Factors:

1. Expected Rate of Return - Higher expected returns increase capital demand

2. Interest Rates - Lower rates make borrowing cheaper, increasing investment

3. Technology Changes - New technology can increase productivity of capital

4. Business Expectations - Optimistic outlook increases investment

Real-World Application: When the Federal Reserve lowers interest rates, firms find it cheaper to borrow money for expansion, leading to increased capital investment across the economy.

üí∞ The Bond Markets

What are Bonds?

Bonds are debt securities where investors lend money to borrowers (governments or corporations) in exchange for periodic interest payments and principal repayment.

Key Bond Characteristics:

Face Value

The amount paid at maturity (e.g., $1,000)

Coupon Rate

Annual interest rate paid to bondholder

Maturity Date

When the principal is repaid

Credit Rating

Risk assessment (AAA to D)

‚öñÔ∏è Inverse Relationship: Bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions. When rates rise, bond prices fall, and vice versa.
Example: A $1,000 bond with 5% coupon pays $50 annually. If market rates rise to 6%, new bonds pay more, making your 5% bond worth less (price drops below $1,000).

üìä The Stock Markets

What are Stocks?

Stocks represent ownership shares in a corporation. Shareholders have claims on company assets and earnings.

Types of Returns:

üìà Capital Gains

Profit from selling stock at higher price than purchase

üíµ Dividends

Portion of profits distributed to shareholders

Stock Valuation Methods:

Present Value Model: Stock value = present value of all future dividends

üåç The Economic Role of Financial Markets

Critical Functions:

1. Capital Allocation
Direct savings from households to productive investments in firms. Markets channel funds to their most valuable uses.
2. Risk Management
Allow investors to diversify and spread risk. Enable risk transfer from those who can't bear it to those who can.
3. Price Discovery
Reveal information about asset values through supply and demand. Prices reflect collective knowledge of market participants.
4. Liquidity Provision
Enable quick conversion of assets to cash. Investors can easily enter and exit positions.
5. Economic Growth
Facilitate capital formation and business expansion. Support innovation and entrepreneurship through funding.
Impact: Efficient financial markets reduce the cost of capital for firms, encouraging investment, job creation, and economic prosperity.
