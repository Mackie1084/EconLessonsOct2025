The fundamental economic problem: unlimited human wants exceed the limited resources available to satisfy them.

üåç Natural Resources

Land, water, minerals, oil

üë• Human Resources

Labor, skills, knowledge

üè≠ Capital Resources

  • Money: Buying a car means less money for other things

üí∞ The Concept of Opportunity Cost

üéØ Opportunity Cost

The value of the next best alternative that you give up when making a choice. It's what you sacrifice to get something else.

üìñ Classic Example: College Decision

Go to College:
Direct costs: $40,000/year
Opportunity cost: $30,000/year (forgone salary)
Total cost: $70,000/year
VS
Work Immediately:
Earn: $30,000/year
Opportunity cost: Future higher earnings
Trade-off decision!

üí° Key Principles:

  • Everything has an opportunity cost - even "free" things
  • Opportunity cost varies by person - different alternatives, different values
  • Consider opportunity cost in all decisions - personal and business
  • Sunk costs don't matter - only future costs and benefits

üèõÔ∏è Economic Systems

Different societies organize their economies in various ways to answer the fundamental economic questions of what, how, and for whom to produce.

üèõÔ∏è Traditional Economy

Based on: Custom, tradition, and ritual

Examples: Rural communities, tribal societies

Pros: Stable, predictable

Cons: Limited growth, inflexible

üìã Command Economy

Based on: Government central planning

Examples: Former Soviet Union, North Korea

Pros: Equal distribution possible

Cons: Inefficient, lacks incentives

üÜì Market Economy

Based on: Supply and demand, private ownership

Examples: Hong Kong, Singapore

Pros: Efficient, innovative

Cons: Income inequality, market failures

‚öñÔ∏è Mixed Economy

Based on: Market forces + government intervention

Examples: USA, Canada, most modern economies

Pros: Balances efficiency and equity

Cons: Can be complex to manage
