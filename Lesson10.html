1. Macroeconomic Models ‚öñÔ∏è

Macroeconomics uses models to analyze the economy as a whole. The two primary models differ mainly in their assumptions about **wage/price flexibility** and the **time horizon**.

  • Classical Model (Long-Run)

    Assumes **flexible** wages and prices. The economy naturally gravitates toward **full employment** because markets always clear. Output is determined by factors of production and technology.

  • Keynesian Model (Short-Run)

    Assumes **sticky** wages and prices. Output and employment are primarily determined by **aggregate demand** (spending), especially during recessions. Government intervention is often necessary.

2. Determining the Economy‚Äôs Output ($\text{Y}$) üè≠

In the **Classical Long-Run Model**, the economy's total output, or **potential GDP** ($\text{Y}$), is determined by the supply-side factors, not by spending.

The Production Function:

$$Y = F(\bar{K}, \bar{L})$$

  • $\bar{K}$ (Capital) and $\bar{L}$ (Labor) are assumed to be **fixed** (or at their natural levels) in the long run.
  • Technology is also fixed.
  • Therefore, the total output ($\text{Y}$) is fixed at the **full-employment level of output** by the available resources. This makes the Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS) curve vertical.

3. The Role of Spending (Aggregate Demand) üõí

While the long-run output ($\text{Y}$) is fixed by supply, **Aggregate Demand ($\text{AD}$)** (total spending) determines **prices and inflation** in the Classical model, not output.

Components of Aggregate Demand:

Total Spending ($\text{E}$) in the economy consists of four main sectors:

  • **Consumption ($\text{C}$):** Household spending on goods and services.
  • **Investment ($\text{I}$):** Firm spending on new capital (factories, equipment).
  • **Government Purchases ($\text{G}$):** Government spending on goods and services.
  • **Net Exports ($\text{NX}$):** Exports minus Imports (assumed zero in early models).

The economy is in equilibrium when **Total Spending ($\text{E}$) = Total Output ($\text{Y}$)**.

4. Total Spending in a Simple Economy ($\text{C} + \text{I} + \text{G}$) üè°

A "simple" closed economy (no trade: $\text{NX}=0$) model focuses on the domestic components of spending. $\text{Y}$ is both **Output** and **Income**.

Equilibrium Condition:

$$Y = C + I + G$$

  • Consumption ($\text{C}$) is a function of **Disposable Income** ($\text{Y} - \text{T}$, where $\text{T}$ is taxes).
  • Investment ($\text{I}$) is a function of the **real interest rate** ($\text{r}$).
  • Government Purchases ($\text{G}$) and Taxes ($\text{T}$) are assumed to be **exogenously fixed** by policy.

5. Total Spending in a Realistic Economy (Open) üö¢

An **Open Economy** includes international trade and finance, adding **Net Exports ($\text{NX}$)** to total spending.

Open Economy Equilibrium:

$$Y = C + I + G + NX$$

  • $\text{NX}$ is determined by the **real exchange rate** and by the difference between national saving and investment.
  • In the long run, net exports must equal **Net Capital Outflow ($\text{NCO}$)**, where $\text{NCO}$ is the difference between domestic saving and domestic investment ($\text{S} - \text{I}$).

Therefore, the equilibrium condition is also seen in the financial markets: $\text{S} = \text{I} + \text{NCO}$.

6. Loanable Funds Market üè¶

The **Loanable Funds Market** is the mechanism that equates the supply of funds (saving) with the demand for funds (investment) via the **real interest rate ($\text{r}$)**.

Market Mechanics:

  • **Supply of Funds (Saving, $\text{S}$):** Comes from **Private Saving** ($\text{Y} - \text{T} - \text{C}$) and **Public Saving** ($\text{T} - \text{G}$). Saving is assumed to be fixed in the long run.
  • **Demand for Funds (Investment, $\text{I}$):** Firms' demand for borrowing to fund new capital projects. Investment is **inversely related** to the real interest rate ($\text{r}$).
  • The real interest rate ($\text{r}$) adjusts to ensure $\text{Saving} = \text{Investment}$.

7. Fiscal Policy: Increase in Government Purchases ($\Delta\text{G}$) üõ°Ô∏è

An increase in government purchases ($\Delta\text{G}$) requires funding, which affects the Loanable Funds Market, specifically **Public Saving** ($\text{T} - \text{G}$).

Impact:

  • **Effect on Saving:** $\Delta\text{G}$ causes **Public Saving to fall** (or the budget deficit to increase). This shifts the **Supply of Loanable Funds** ($\text{S}$) curve **left**.
  • **Effect on Interest Rate:** The leftward shift in supply drives the **real interest rate ($\text{r}$) up**.
  • **Crowding Out:** The higher $\text{r}$ discourages private **Investment ($\text{I}$)**. The decrease in $\text{I}$ offsets the increase in $\text{G}$. This is known as **Crowding Out**. Output ($\text{Y}$) remains fixed.

8. Fiscal Policy: Decrease in Net Taxes ($\Delta\text{T}$) üìâ

A decrease in net taxes ($\Delta\text{T}$) also impacts the Loanable Funds Market and the economy's equilibrium.

Impact:

  • **Effect on Saving:** $\Delta\text{T}$ (tax cut) reduces **Public Saving** ($\text{T} - \text{G}$). It also increases **Private Saving** ($\text{Y} - \text{T} - \text{C}$) as disposable income rises, but the net effect on total $\text{S}$ is negative. The Supply curve shifts **left**.
  • **Effect on Interest Rate:** The reduction in total saving drives the **real interest rate ($\text{r}$) up**.
  • **Crowding Out:** The higher $\text{r}$ reduces private **Investment ($\text{I}$)**. Just like with $\Delta\text{G}$, the fiscal policy action crowds out private investment. Output ($\text{Y}$) remains fixed at the full-employment level.
