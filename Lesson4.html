1. Perfect Competition

Perfect competition represents an idealized market structure where multiple conditions create an environment of maximum efficiency.

Key Characteristics:

  • Many buyers and sellers: No single participant can influence market price
  • Homogeneous products: Products are identical across all sellers
  • Perfect information: All participants have complete knowledge of prices and products
  • Free entry and exit: No barriers prevent firms from entering or leaving the market
  • Price takers: Firms must accept the market-determined price
üí° Key Insight: In perfect competition, firms earn zero economic profit in the long run due to free entry and exit. This doesn't mean accounting profit is zero‚Äîit means firms earn just enough to cover all costs including opportunity costs.
üìå Real-World Example: Agricultural markets (wheat, corn) come closest to perfect competition. Individual farmers cannot influence the market price and sell nearly identical products.

2. Competitive Firm

A competitive firm operates under the constraints of perfect competition and must make optimal decisions to maximize profit.

Profit Maximization Rule:

P = MC (Price = Marginal Cost)
A competitive firm maximizes profit by producing where price equals marginal cost, as long as price covers average variable cost.

Key Decision Points:

  • Production Level: Produce where P = MC
  • Shutdown Decision: Shut down if P < AVC (average variable cost)
  • Exit Decision: Exit market if P < ATC (average total cost) in long run

3. Competitive Markets in Short-Run

In the short run, at least one factor of production is fixed, and firms may earn positive, negative, or zero economic profit.

Short-Run Possibilities:

  • Economic Profit (P > ATC): Firm earns above-normal returns
  • Normal Profit (P = ATC): Firm covers all costs including opportunity costs
  • Economic Loss (P < ATC but P > AVC): Firm loses money but continues operating
  • Shutdown (P < AVC): Firm temporarily ceases production

Short-Run Equilibrium

Price and quantity are determined by market supply and demand intersection

Individual Firm: Takes market price as given

Market: Determines equilibrium price and quantity

‚è±Ô∏è Short-Run Dynamics: Firms cannot adjust all inputs (like factory size), so they optimize production with existing capacity. This leads to varying profit levels across firms.

4. Competitive Markets in Long-Run

In the long run, all factors of production are variable, and the entry/exit of firms drives economic profit to zero.

Long-Run Adjustment Process:

If Economic Profit > 0:
New firms enter ‚Üí Supply increases ‚Üí Price falls ‚Üí Profit decreases to zero
If Economic Loss < 0:
Firms exit ‚Üí Supply decreases ‚Üí Price rises ‚Üí Loss decreases to zero

Long-Run Equilibrium Conditions:

  • P = MC (profit maximization)
  • P = ATC (zero economic profit)
  • Firms produce at minimum ATC (productive efficiency)
  • P = MC (allocative efficiency)
üéØ Efficiency Achievement: Perfect competition achieves both productive efficiency (minimum ATC) and allocative efficiency (P = MC), maximizing total surplus in society.

5. Overview of Monopoly

A monopoly is a market structure where a single seller controls the entire market for a product with no close substitutes.

Characteristics of Monopoly:

  • Single seller: One firm supplies the entire market
  • No close substitutes: Product is unique
  • Price maker: Firm can set price (faces downward-sloping demand)
  • High barriers to entry: Other firms cannot easily enter

Sources of Monopoly Power:

Legal Barriers

Patents, copyrights, licenses

Natural Monopoly

High fixed costs, economies of scale

Control of Resources

Exclusive ownership of key inputs

Network Effects

Value increases with more users

üìå Examples: Local utility companies (natural monopoly), pharmaceutical patents (legal monopoly), Microsoft Windows in 1990s (network effects)

6. Monopoly Behavior

Monopolies behave differently from competitive firms because they can influence market price.

Key Differences from Competition:

  • Price > MC: Monopolies charge above marginal cost
  • Lower output: Produce less than competitive markets
  • Deadweight loss: Create inefficiency in the market
  • Profit maximization: MR = MC (not P = MC)
üìä Important Relationship:
For a monopoly: Marginal Revenue < Price

This occurs because to sell more units, the monopolist must lower price on all units sold, not just the additional unit.

Monopoly Pricing Strategy:

  1. Find quantity where MR = MC
  2. Go up to demand curve to find price
  3. Price will be higher than MC
  4. Create consumer surplus loss and deadweight loss

7. Equilibrium in Monopoly Markets

Monopoly equilibrium results in higher prices, lower quantities, and deadweight loss compared to perfect competition.

Monopoly Equilibrium Conditions:

  • MR = MC: Profit-maximizing output level
  • P > MC: Price exceeds marginal cost
  • Economic profit > 0: Can persist in long run due to barriers
  • No allocative efficiency: P ‚â† MC creates deadweight loss

8. Price Discrimination

Price discrimination occurs when a firm charges different prices to different customers for the same product, based on willingness to pay.

Types of Price Discrimination:

First-Degree (Perfect Price Discrimination)

Charge each customer their maximum willingness to pay. Captures all consumer surplus. Example: Negotiated car prices, custom art commissions

Second-Degree (Quantity Discrimination)

Different prices based on quantity purchased. Example: Bulk discounts, utility pricing tiers, "buy 2 get 1 free"

Third-Degree (Market Segmentation)

Different prices for different groups. Example: Student discounts, senior citizen rates, regional pricing

Requirements for Price Discrimination:

  • Market power (ability to set prices)
  • Ability to identify different customer groups
  • Prevention of resale (arbitrage)
üí∞ Profit Impact: Price discrimination allows monopolies to capture more consumer surplus as profit, increasing total revenue compared to single pricing.

9. Concepts of Imperfect Competition

Imperfect competition describes market structures between perfect competition and monopoly, where firms have some but not complete market power.

Key Features:

  • Price-setting ability: Firms face downward-sloping demand curves
  • Product differentiation: Products are similar but not identical
  • Some barriers to entry: Entry is possible but not completely free
  • Strategic behavior: Firms consider competitors' actions

10. Monopolistic Competition

Monopolistic competition features many firms selling differentiated products, combining elements of both competition and monopoly.

Characteristics:

  • Many sellers: Numerous firms in the market
  • Differentiated products: Similar but not identical goods
  • Free entry/exit: Low barriers to entry
  • Non-price competition: Heavy use of advertising and branding

Short-Run vs Long-Run:

Short-Run:

Firms can earn positive or negative economic profit. Price > ATC is possible due to product differentiation and brand loyalty.

Long-Run:

Entry/exit drives profit to zero (like perfect competition). However, P > MC persists (like monopoly), creating excess capacity and inefficiency.

Efficiency Analysis:

  • Not productively efficient: Don't produce at minimum ATC
  • Not allocatively efficient: P > MC creates deadweight loss
  • Benefit: Product variety benefits consumers
üìå Examples: Restaurants, clothing stores, hair salons, coffee shops, gas stations‚Äîmarkets with many competitors offering similar but differentiated products.

11. Oligopoly

Oligopoly is a market structure dominated by a few large firms that are interdependent and engage in strategic behavior.

Key Characteristics:

  • Few sellers: Market controlled by small number of firms
  • Interdependence: Each firm's decisions affect others
  • Barriers to entry: Significant obstacles prevent new competition
  • Possible product differentiation: Can be homogeneous or differentiated

Strategic Behavior Models:

Game Theory: Oligopolists use strategic thinking to predict and respond to competitors' actions. The prisoner's dilemma illustrates why cooperation is difficult.

Collusion vs Competition:

Collusion (Cartel): Firms cooperate to act like a monopoly, maximizing joint profits. Example: OPEC

Competition: Firms compete aggressively, driving prices toward competitive levels but above marginal cost.

Types of Oligopoly:

  • Cournot Model: Firms choose quantities simultaneously
  • Bertrand Model: Firms choose prices simultaneously
  • Stackelberg Model: One firm leads, others follow
  • Kinked Demand Curve: Explains price rigidity in oligopolies
üìå Examples: Automobile manufacturers, airline industry, telecommunications, soft drink market (Coca-Cola vs Pepsi), smartphone operating systems
‚öñÔ∏è Regulatory Concern: Oligopolies can lead to reduced competition, higher prices, and decreased consumer welfare. Antitrust laws attempt to prevent collusion and promote competition.
