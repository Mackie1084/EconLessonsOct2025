üè™ Overview of Markets

üéØ What is a Market?

A market is any arrangement that enables buyers and sellers to get information and do business with each other. Markets don't need a physical location - they can exist anywhere buyers and sellers interact!

üîÑ How Markets Work:

üõí Buyers (Demand)

üåê Types of Markets:

  • Perfect Competition: Many buyers and sellers, identical products
  • Monopolistic Competition: Many sellers, differentiated products
  • Oligopoly: Few large sellers dominate the market
  • Monopoly: Single seller controls the entire market

üîÑ Product and Resource Markets

The economy consists of two main types of markets that are interconnected in a continuous flow of goods, services, and money.

üõí Product Markets

What: Goods and services sold to consumers

Who: Businesses sell, Households buy

Examples: Grocery stores, restaurants, online retailers

üè≠ Resource Markets

What: Labor, land, and capital

Who: Households sell, Businesses buy

Examples: Job markets, real estate, stock exchanges

üîÑ Circular Flow Model:

Real Money Flow:

Product Market: Households pay money ‚Üí Businesses for goods/services

Resource Market: Businesses pay money ‚Üí Households for labor/resources

Real Goods/Services Flow:

Product Market: Businesses provide goods/services ‚Üí Households

Resource Market: Households provide labor/resources ‚Üí Businesses

üõí Overview of Demand

üìñ What is Demand?

Demand is the quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to buy at various prices during a specific time period.

üîë Key Components of Demand:

üí∞ Willingness

Consumers want the product

üí≥ Ability

Consumers can afford it

üìä Quantity

Specific amount at specific price

üìà Factors That Affect Demand:

  • Price of the good: Primary factor (creates movement along curve)
  • Consumer income: More income typically means more demand
  • Prices of related goods: Substitutes and complements
  • Consumer tastes/preferences: Fashion, trends, advertising
  • Future expectations: Expected price changes or income changes
  • Number of buyers: Population size and demographics

‚öñÔ∏è Law of Demand

üìè The Law of Demand

As the price of a good increases, the quantity demanded decreases, and vice versa, all other factors being equal (ceteris paribus).

ü§î Why Does This Happen?

üí∏ Substitution Effect

Higher price ‚Üí switch to cheaper alternatives

üí∞ Income Effect

Higher price ‚Üí reduced purchasing power

üìâ Diminishing Utility

Each additional unit provides less satisfaction

üçï Real-World Example: Pizza

At $5/slice: You might buy 4 slices (you're hungry!)

At $15/slice: You might buy 1 slice (too expensive!)

At $25/slice: You buy 0 slices (eat something else!)


Result: Higher price ‚Üí Lower quantity demanded

üè≠ Law of Supply

üìè The Law of Supply

As the price of a good increases, the quantity supplied increases, and vice versa, all other factors being equal (ceteris paribus).

ü§î Why Do Producers Supply More at Higher Prices?

üí∞ Higher Profits

More revenue per unit sold

üìà Cover Costs

Higher prices can cover higher production costs

üè≠ Incentive to Expand

Profitable to increase production

üìä Factors That Affect Supply:

  • Price of the good: Primary factor (movement along curve)
  • Production costs: Labor, materials, energy costs
  • Technology: Better technology reduces costs
